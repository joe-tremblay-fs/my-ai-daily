Microsoft

Company
Build 2026 MAI models — corrected sizing (Simon Willison)
⭐ Significant Correction
Jun 2, 2026 — Willison post updated
An important fix to how this brief (and others) described the Build models. Per Willison's corrected notes, the active-parameter counts were misread as totals: MAI-Thinking-1 is a 1T-parameter MoE with 35B active (not a 35B dense model), and MAI-Code-1-Flash is 137B with 5B active (purpose-built for GitHub Copilot / VS Code). Microsoft claims MAI-Thinking-1 "is preferred to Sonnet 4.6 in blind human side-by-side evaluations." And the "appropriately licensed data" claim doesn't hold up: the technical paper (from p.80) describes a proprietary crawl of ~1.2T pages (filtered to 794B) plus Common Crawl (24.2B pages) — "the same licensing problems as all of the other major LLMs."

Notable Themes

The frontier labs are now critical infrastructure — and outages prove it

Friday's two-hour Claude outage took down claude.ai, the API, Claude Code, and Cowork at once, and the immediate worry wasn't downtime but a possible cross-user data leak (responses surfacing for the wrong user). Whether or not that claim holds, the reaction shows how far the dependency has gone: a single provider's infrastructure hiccup ripples through coding pipelines and agent workflows industry-wide. "Reckoning with AI's increasing status as infrastructure," as Thoughtworks put it.

Trust, but verify the spec sheet

Simon Willison's correction on the MAI models is a small but instructive episode: even a careful, well-sourced observer misread MoE active-parameter counts as totals (MAI-Thinking-1 is 1T/35B-active, not 35B), and Microsoft's "appropriately licensed data" turned out to be the usual proprietary-crawl-plus-Common-Crawl mix once the technical paper was read closely. As model announcements get denser and more marketing-shaped, the gap between the headline and the model card is where the real story sits.

Inference cost is now a research agenda, not just a budget line

Today's only new HN paper ("Latent Agents" — internalize multi-agent debate into weights to skip the runtime cost) sits alongside this week's KVarN KV-cache quantization, the QKV-projection-variants study, GitHub's token-efficiency-in-agentic-workflows post, and Microsoft's small-active-parameter MAI models. Different layers — architecture, serving, workflow, model design — all aimed at the same target: making agent-scale inference cheaper.

Knowledge management is the next agent frontier

Karpathy's spreading "LLM Wiki" pattern (an agent maintaining a backlinked Markdown knowledge base, ~95% token savings in one case) and O'Reilly's recurring agent-memory pieces point the same way: once you have capable agents, the bottleneck shifts from generating output to retaining and organizing context across sessions. The interesting bet is that plain structured files beat vector-DB RAG for a lot of this.

A genuinely quiet weekend — and the signal is the absence

Two days running, no lab shipped anything new and practitioner output thinned to almost nothing. The durable signal this week wasn't a model launch but practitioner sense-making (containment, cost, open-source trust, skill) and, today, a reliability scare. After a Jun 1–3 wave of announcements, the system is catching its breath — worth noting before next week likely refills the pipeline.
