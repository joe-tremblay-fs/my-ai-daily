Policy on the AI Exponential — Advanced AI Framework + Economic Policy Framework
⭐ Significant New
Jun 10, 2026 — anthropic.com
Anthropic published two policy proposals arguing "the policymaking process was built for a slower world." The Advanced AI Framework proposes that government get legal authority to block or deter the deployment of models that pose a significant risk of catastrophic harm — "beyond what exists in current law or in existing proposals in Congress" — with civil penalties tied to global annual revenue that escalate with repeated violations. It would apply only to models trained using >10²⁵ FLOPs, developed by companies earning >$500M in AI revenue or spending >$1B on AI R&D, and addresses four catastrophic-risk categories: biological, cyber, loss of control, and automated AI R&D. Requirements for frontier developers: mandatory testing + published summaries, a public safety framework and system cards, regular risk reports, independent third-party evaluation, and a security program for weights/infrastructure. On preemption: Congress should not preempt state law "unless it enacts a federal law that is at least as strong." It cites Claude Mythos Preview discovering thousands of high-severity vulnerabilities (in every major OS and browser) as evidence the capability trend is real. The Economic Policy Framework addresses worker/economic preparation and broadly sharing AI's financial benefits. Lands the day after the Fable/Mythos launch and the same week as the silent-interventions reversal — a coordinated push to frame Anthropic as pro-regulation.

Google DeepMind

Company
Investing in multi-agent AI safety research — up to $10M funding call
⭐ Significant New
Jun 11, 2026 — deepmind.google (with Schmidt Sciences, Cooperative AI Foundation, ARIA, Google.org)
DeepMind and partners opened a global technical research funding call of up to $10M focused on a gap most safety work ignores: how large populations of independently-built agents behave when they "communicate, negotiate and transact with one another." The argument: "Most safety evaluations analyze models in isolation," but interacting agents can produce sudden "emergent" collective behaviors — "an unpredictable flurry of economic activity" or new security failure modes — that current tools can't predict, measure, or monitor. Four priority areas: sandboxes/testbeds (virtual marketplaces, simulated ecosystems), the science of agent networks (how collective capabilities emerge and how networks fail or become volatile), strengthening agent infrastructure (identity, reputation, commitment protocols for cross-platform interaction), and oversight and control (monitoring deployed agent populations). Deadline Aug 8, 2026, awardees in Autumn 2026. Directly complements the week's recurring "multi-agent / harness" theme (O'Reilly's delegation problem, Squad, /fleet) — and lands the same day OpenAI/Anthropic governance debates intensify.
DiffusionGemma: open-weight diffusion text model, "4x faster" generation
New
Jun 10, 2026 — blog.google / Hugging Face (also covered by Simon Willison)
Google revived its May 2025 experimental "Gemini Diffusion" research as a shipping open-weight (Apache 2) Gemma model: google/diffusiongemma-26B-A4B-it (a 26B / 4B-active MoE). Diffusion-based text generation produces tokens in parallel rather than left-to-right, hence the speed claim — NVIDIA is hosting it free on the NIM API, where Simon Willison clocked it at 500+ tokens/second (2,409 tokens in 4.4s) generating his standard pelican-on-a-bicycle SVG. Notable as (a) a rare open-weight release from Google in a week otherwise dominated by closed frontier models, and (b) a bet on diffusion as a faster decoding paradigm for text.

A 24-hour governance reversal: community pressure is now a fast feedback loop on model policy

The meta-point matters more than the specifics: undocumented model behavior, once surfaced, is now subject to a rapid public-accountability cycle — and labs are responding on the timescale of a news cycle, not a release cycle.

Anthropic is running a deliberate pro-regulation play — while OpenAI stays quiet

In the same week it shipped the most capable model yet, Anthropic published "Policy on the AI Exponential," explicitly asking governments for authority to block or deter dangerous deployments, revenue-scaled penalties, mandatory third-party evaluation, and a 10²⁵-FLOP / $500M-revenue threshold. This is a notably harder line than OpenAI's May 28 Frontier Governance Framework — and OpenAI has said nothing about either Fable or the policy push. The contrast is becoming a positioning axis: Anthropic foregrounds safety-and-regulation as differentiation; OpenAI foregrounds capability and distribution. Today's silent-interventions reversal is the same instinct turned inward.

Multi-agent safety graduates from blog theme to funded research agenda

The "harness over base model" and "delegation problem" discussions of the past two weeks now have institutional money behind them: DeepMind's up-to-$10M call (with Schmidt Sciences, the Cooperative AI Foundation, and ARIA) targets the behavior of populations of interacting agents — emergent collective dynamics, network fragility, identity/reputation protocols, population-level oversight. The gap the call names — "most safety evaluations analyze models in isolation" — is exactly the gap the Fedora story illustrates. Expect agent-ecosystem safety to be a durable, fundable topic.

DiffusionGemma (Apache 2, 26B/4B-active, 500+ tok/s, free on NVIDIA NIM) is a reminder that even in a week defined by a $10/$50-per-M closed Mythos-class model, the open-weight floor keeps rising — and exploring new decoding paradigms (parallel diffusion vs. autoregressive).
