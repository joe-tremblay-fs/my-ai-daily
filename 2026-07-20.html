Notable Themes

Cross-cutting observations

  • Trillion-scale is the new entry ticket — and it's a Chinese-lab + xAI story. In three days: Kimi K3 (2.8T open), Qwen3.8-Max (2.4T, open "soon"), and a teased Grok 4.6 (2T). Every one is framed against Fable 5 or Kimi K3, not against each other's benchmarks. The Western frontier labs are conspicuously quiet — Gemini 3.5 Pro still unshipped, no new OpenAI or Anthropic model — so the raw-scale headlines are being written in Shanghai and at Colossus 2.
  • "Preview" now means a purchasable teaser with no numbers. Qwen3.8 is live and billable at 10% pricing, yet ships with no benchmark table, model card, license, or active-parameter count — the one figure that determines whether a 2.4T MoE is actually cheap to serve. The pattern (announce scale, defer verification) is becoming the standard Chinese-lab launch cadence, and practitioners are learning to wait for the Hugging Face repo before believing the ranking.
  • Capacity, not capability, keeps being the binding constraint. Anthropic reinstating Fable at 50% of limits, Simon Willison wondering aloud whether that steals training GPUs, and Moonshot pausing Kimi K3 signups amid a GPU shortage are the same story from three angles: the models exist, but serving them at flat-rate scale is where the real fight is. Trillion-parameter bragging rights collide with H200 arithmetic.
  • The open-weights flood has an ironic historical footnote. Simon Willison's surfaced 2022 Altman email — releasing a local model to "discourage others" and "make it harder for new efforts to get funded" — reads very differently in a week when Chinese labs are open-weighting trillion-parameter frontier models precisely to erode the closed labs' moat. Open release as competitive weapon cuts both ways.
  • Practitioners are pre-registering doubt about their own playbook. Mollick ("not convinced these approaches are durable"), the "AI Mania" field report, and LessWrong's "current AIs seem pretty misaligned" all push the same caution: the harness-and-orchestration recipes and the executive enthusiasm of mid-2026 are provisional, and a single model jump — or a single honest benchmark — could reset them.
