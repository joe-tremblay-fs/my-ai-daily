Addy Osmani — addyo.substack.com (Elevate)
Loop Engineering — from prompting agents to designing the systems that prompt them
New
Jun 8, 2026 — Substack (Elevate)
Today's one genuinely fresh practitioner post. Osmani's thesis: stop being the person who prompts the agent and instead design the system that does — "a loop is a recursive goal where you define a purpose and the AI iterates until complete." He cites Peter Steinberger ("You should be designing loops that prompt your agents") and Anthropic's Boris Cherny ("I don't prompt Claude anymore… My job is to write loops"). The pattern is five building blocks that both Claude Code and Codex now ship: (1) automations on a schedule for discovery/triage — incl. Claude Code's /loop (re-run on a cadence) and /goal (run until a verifiable stop condition, with a separate model grading "done"); (2) worktrees for parallel-agent isolation; (3) skills (SKILL.md) to capture project knowledge; (4) plugins/connectors (MCP) so the loop acts in real tools; (5) sub-agents to split maker from checker — plus a sixth, on-disk memory. He stays pointedly skeptical: token cost varies wildly, verification is still on you, and a smooth loop accelerates comprehension debt and "cognitive surrender." Closing line: "Build the loop. But build it like someone who intends to stay the engineer, not just the person who presses go." Directly extends this week's reviewing-and-steering-agents theme, and his own prior posts (agent harness engineering, the factory model, long-running agents, adversarial code review).

Notable Themes

Open weights are crowding the frontier — at a fraction of the price

DeepSeek V4-Pro landing near GPT-5.5 on hard benchmarks (80.6% SWE-bench Verified, 90.1% GPQA Diamond) at ~$0.435/M tokens is the clearest signal yet that the capability gap between open and closed models is narrowing while the price gap stays enormous. It slots directly into the cost story the week has been telling — Uber's $1,500/tool cap, GitHub's token billing, Microsoft's small-active-parameter MAI models, the Agent SDK billing change. If an open model is "good enough" for most agent workloads at a tenth of the cost, the budget-pressure conversation gets a new and disruptive option.

The skill-degradation worry is now spawning counter-tools

Last week four sources independently asked whether leaning on AI erodes human judgment. This week the response is becoming concrete: Lathe explicitly uses LLMs to make you learn a domain rather than skip it, inverting the cognitive-offloading default. Pair that with Tornhill's "implementation was never just typing — it's discovery and learning" and Muratori's "Will AI Make Me Worse?", and you can see a design movement forming around preserving the learning that execution used to force.

Reviewing and steering agent output is the quiet operational story

Raschka's empirical "do AGENTS.md files actually help?" sits alongside AWS's "How Baz improved its AI Agent Code Review accuracy," GitHub's "Agent pull requests are everywhere — here's how to review them," and the SQLite/Ladybird use of AGENTS.md to reject contributions. The common thread: now that agents generate code at volume, the bottleneck and the open questions have moved to how you steer, validate, and review their output — and how much of the steering we do actually works.

A postmortem-shaped silence at Anthropic

Three days after the Jun 5 outage and data-leak claims, there's still no published root-cause analysis. For a provider now functioning as critical infrastructure, the absence of an incident report is itself worth tracking: it leaves the cross-user-leak question formally open and tests how transparent a soon-to-be-public company will be about reliability. Watch for whether a postmortem lands this week.

Three quiet days — the lab pipeline is genuinely paused

From Jun 6 through Jun 8, not one of the five tracked labs shipped anything new, and practitioner output thinned to near zero. After the dense Jun 1–3 wave (Series H/S-1, Bedrock GA, Build's MAI models, the recursive self-improvement report), the system is clearly catching its breath. The signal this stretch came almost entirely from outside the labs — an open-weight benchmark run and practitioner sense-making — which is itself a useful reminder that not every important development originates in a US frontier lab's newsroom.
