AI and Liability — quoting Bruce Schneier on the German Google ruling
New
Jun 25, 2026 · 10:28 pm — quote / link blog
Simon highlights Bruce Schneier on a landmark German ruling declaring that Google's AI Overviews are "Google's own words," making Google liable for false answers they produce. Schneier's framing: "AI agents are agents of the person or organization that deploys them — and should be treated by the law as such." If a company would be liable for inaccuracies written by human staff, it should be equally liable for its AI's output; letting businesses "hide behind the excuse of faulty AI" would be "a massive handout to companies" and a perverse incentive to replace accountable humans with unaccountable models. A clean legal counterpart to the month's accountability/verification thread.

Notable Themes

Cross-cutting observations

  • "Agents over chatbots" now has numbers behind it. OpenAI's Codex paper is the most concrete public data yet for a claim the field has been asserting all year: the unit of knowledge work is shifting from short chat turns to long-horizon, delegated tasks. 99.8% of OpenAI's output tokens flowing through an agent, and 99th-percentile users orchestrating 60+ agent-hours/day, are striking even allowing for OpenAI being the most extreme early adopter of its own tool. It's the supply-side mirror of O'Reilly's "long-running agents" and Pragmatic Engineer's "slow down to speed up."
  • Non-developers are the real growth story. The 137×/189× non-developer adoption figures, and the finding that a quarter of business-function Codex work is coding, reframe "agentic coding tools" as general knowledge-work tools that happen to write code. That widens the addressable market — and the disruption — well beyond engineering.
  • Liability is becoming the hard edge of AI accountability. The German Google AI Overviews ruling (via Schneier/Willison) crystallizes a principle that pairs with Anthropic's Alibaba extraction claim and the export-control fights: as models get embedded in real decisions and products, the legal system is starting to assign responsibility to whoever deploys them. "Blame the AI" is being explicitly rejected as a defense.
  • Cheap generation keeps swamping human review channels. Today added the science.org medical-student paper-mill story to the running list — Greptile PR spam, Tom MacWright's LLM job applications, Filippo Valsorda's vuln-report fatigue. Across code review, hiring, vuln triage and now academic publishing, the bottleneck and the trust signal are migrating to whoever has to read the output.
  • Slow news days are getting rarer — and this is one. After a fortnight of near-daily frontier launches, custom-silicon reveals and IP disputes, Jun 26 is genuinely quiet: one substantive company post, one practitioner item, and an HN front page that barely turned over. Worth noting precisely because it's unusual for this stretch.
